Bangabandhu Sheikh Mujibur Rahman

1920 - 1975

The Father of the Nation

Sheikh Mujibur Rahman is affectionately known as the "Father of the Nation" in Bangladesh for his pivotal role in leading the Bengali people to independence and establishing the sovereign state of Bangladesh. As the leader of the Awami League, he emerged as a prominent figure in the struggle for Bengali autonomy, advocating for the rights of the Bengali population against the oppressive regime of West Pakistan. His historic speech on March 7, 1971, galvanized the nation and ignited the movement for independence. Following the landslide victory of the Awami League in the 1970 general elections, Sheikh Mujibur Rahman became the symbol of Bengali aspirations for self-determination. Despite the refusal of the ruling authorities to transfer power, he led the Bengali population in their armed struggle for independence, ultimately culminating in the birth of Bangladesh. His leadership during the liberation war earned international recognition and support, solidifying his legacy as a unifying figure and a symbol of Bangladesh's journey to freedom. Even after independence, Sheikh Mujibur Rahman's vision of a democratic, secular, and prosperous Bangladesh continues to inspire generations, cementing his status as the revered "Father of the Nation."

Early Life

Mujib was born in 1920 into the Bengali Muslim aristocratic Sheikh family of the village of Tungipara in Gopalganj sub-division of Faridpur district in the province of Bengal in British India.His father Sheikh Lutfur Rahman was a sheristadar (law clerk) in the courthouse of Gopalganj; Mujib's mother Sheikh Sayera Khatun was a housewife. Mujib's father Sheikh Lutfur Rahman was a Taluqdar in Tungipara, owning landed property, around 100 Bighas of cultivable land. His clan's ancestors were Zamindars of Faridpur Mahakumar, however due to successive turns in the family fortune over generations had turned them middle class. The Sheikh clan of Tungipara were of Iraqi Arab descent, being descended from Sheikh Abdul Awal Darwish of Baghdad, who had come to preach Islam in the Mughal era.[16] His lineage is; Sheikh Mujibur Rahman, son of Sheikh Lutfar Rahman son of, Sheikh Abdul Hamid, son of Sheikh Mohammad Zakir, son of Sheikh Ekramullah, son of Sheikh Borhanuddin, son of Sheikh Jan Mahmud, son of Sheikh Zahiruddin, son of Sheikh Abdul Awal Darwish. Mujib was the third child in a family of four daughters and two sons. His parents nicknamed him "Khoka".

Death of Sheikh Mujib; a horrifying day!

Sheikh Mujibur Rahman, the founding leader of Bangladesh, met a tragic end on August 15, 1975. A group of military officers carried out a coup d'état, resulting in his assassination along with most of his family members at his residence in Dhaka. This event, known as the 1975 Bangladeshi coup d'état, shook the nation and plunged it into political turmoil. The death of Sheikh Mujibur Rahman marked a dark chapter in Bangladesh's history, leading to significant political instability and a series of subsequent military coups before the country eventually transitioned back to democratic governance.The aftermath of Sheikh Mujibur Rahman's assassination saw Bangladesh endure a series of military coups and authoritarian rule. The country struggled to find stability and democratic governance in the years following his death. The legacy of Sheikh Mujibur Rahman, his vision for a democratic and prosperous Bangladesh, was overshadowed by the turmoil and instability that ensued after his assassination. In later years, Bangladesh managed to overcome the political upheaval and transition back to democratic governance. Sheikh Mujibur Rahman's memory and contributions to the nation remained deeply ingrained in the collective consciousness of Bangladeshis. His death serves as a reminder of the fragility of democracy and the enduring struggle for political stability and social justice in the nation's history.
